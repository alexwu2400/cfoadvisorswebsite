Navigating Uncertain Waters: Learning from the Washington Mutual Crisis to Safeguard Silicon Valley Bank Stakeholders

‍

There's been well-warranted panic around the developing situation facing Silicon Valley Bank. While no one knows for sure how this will play out, it can be helpful to look at previous bank runs to understand possible timelines and how founders should best prepare‍


--


Washington Mutual’s Bank run and Receivership


During the 2008 financial crisis, one of the most notable bank runs was Washington Mutual. The company held assets of $327.9 billion, making it one of the largest banks in the US. However, a credit downgrade led to a sudden bank run, with customers withdrawing their deposits en masse. This ultimately resulted in WaMu's placement into receivership and its assets and deposits being acquired by JPM. In comparison, Silicon Valley Bank holds assets valued at $209.0 billion, and is facing a similar situation.


Timeline and Acquisition by J.P. Morgan Chase

  • Sept 15, 2008: The holding company receives a credit rating agency downgrade.
  • Sept 16-24, 2008: In the following week, WaMu experiences a bank run, with customers withdrawing $16.7 billion in deposits.
  • Sept 25, 2008: The Office of Thrift Supervision closes WaMu and places it under receivership.
  • Sept 25, 2008: JPM acquires select banking operations of Washington Mutual Bank in a transaction facilitated by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation. https://lnkd.in/gTA8Si6X

Outcome for Depositors

After JPM takes over WaMu's operations, all deposit accounts, which include Checking, Savings, Money Market, CDs, Brokered Accounts and Retirement accounts were transferred to JPMorgan Chase. All depositors were made whole.


Key Takeaways and Advice for Founders

While every black swan situation is different, there are key takeaways that founders can learn from past bank runs to inform their response to the current situation faced by Silicon Valley Bank.

  • The FDIC has a track record of quickly facilitating asset acquisitions to provide liquidity. This provides some hopeful optimism that depositors will be made whole. Speed will depend on how quickly acquirers can liquidate SVB's balance sheet.
  • However, Founders should also take proactive measures to preserve cash on hand and maximize working capital in the meantime to avoid technical default on any debt and ensure that vulnerable employees are paid on time.
  • Avoid getting caught up in mania and speculation. Focus on facts and historical data to guide your decision-making process. Do not make hasty decisions that may negatively impact your company in the long run.
  • There may be an increase in predatory working capital financing offers to founders during this time of crisis. Before signing exploitative financing terms, founders should understand that additional clarity in these situations have been provided quickly.
